3.

Juniper's Clompass

Re-enchanting learning through analog-digital hybridity

Executive Summary

This case study explores how hands-on learning environments strengthen cognitive flexibility and spatial reasoning in children. Juniper’s Compass is a hybrid physical-digital curriculum that invites students to connect curiosity, navigation, and problem-solving through embodied play. The project positions curriculum design as trust-by-design—balancing agency, discovery, and guided reflection in early STEM education.

Core Question

How can children learn to orient themselves—mentally, emotionally, and spatially—through design-based inquiry rather than instruction?

Domain Creative Literacies · Technical Making · Curiosity-Driven Learning

Merging storytelling, writing, and electronics to create hybrid learning experiences.

Experience Analog clock → digital compass

Hands-on exploration that encourages learners to orient themselves through story and sky.

Toolkit Arduino · Magnetometer · LCD

Modular electronics coupled with narrative prompts for classroom tinkering.

Curriculum Co-designed lesson plans

Tested with K–5 learners to strengthen spatial reasoning and creative writing.

The Challenge

Educators observed that students struggled with persistence and confidence when facing ambiguous, open-ended problems. Traditional STEM curricula celebrated correct answers over process reflection, leaving little room for students to grapple with uncertainty.

Design Hypothesis

If children could “see” problem-solving as navigation rather than evaluation, they could transfer those skills across emotional, cognitive, and spatial domains.

Concept Development

Design process +

Concept: Traditional education separates creative writing from technical building. The Clompass fuses both—students build a compass and use it to navigate stories embedded in physical, directional space.

Technical + creative integration: Vintage clock + Arduino + magnetometer. Compass directions linked to narrative elements (North = beginning, East = adventure). Writing prompts emerge from physical orientation.

Key insight: Students wanted to understand magnetism because it unlocked story possibilities. Curiosity drove learning across both domains.

Design Decisions

Learning Framework

Juniper’s Compass integrates embodied cognition and maker pedagogy. Students manipulate materials—gears, magnets, and recycled parts—to build personal “compass” artifacts that represent how they orient to challenges.

Technology Integration

  • Physical prototyping with 3D-printed parts and Arduino sensors (light and tilt)
  • Simple coding tasks introducing coordinate systems and directionality
  • Optional digital layer using Scratch for virtual compass visualization

Pedagogical Structure

  1. Inquire: Students explore “What helps me find my way?” through journaling and discussion.
  2. Build: Teams design and prototype navigational tools using recycled materials.
  3. Reflect: Learners map moments of being “stuck” and identify strategies for redirection.
  4. Iterate: Students present how their compass represents resilience.

Curriculum Framework

Stakeholder Role Access Learning Intent
Student Builder / Researcher Designs and tests prototypes Develops agency and reflection
Teacher Facilitator / Observer Provides prompts, documents process Shifts from instructor to design coach
System Curriculum Layer Collects qualitative data on iteration patterns Informs adaptive scaffolds and equitable assessment

Key Principle: Learning happens through navigation, not correction. The compass becomes both metaphor and method for regulating attention, emotion, and collaboration.

User Research & Insights

Participants: 50 students (Grades 3–5) and 5 teachers in mixed-language classrooms.

Methods: Observation, think-alouds, student journaling, and teacher reflection logs.

  • Students who typically disengaged showed increased persistence when “navigation” language replaced “right/wrong.”
  • Peer-to-peer guidance emerged naturally, strengthening empathy and collaboration.
  • Teachers observed stronger articulation of how problems were solved rather than whether they were solved.

Design implication: Curriculum should make uncertainty visible and playable.

Outcomes & Next Steps

What Worked

  • Navigation metaphor improved cognitive resilience and peer collaboration.
  • Hands-on and digital integration engaged diverse learners.
  • Framework now informs adaptive modules in Unii Learning Labs’ product roadmap.

Open Questions

  • How might the navigation framework translate to digital learning platforms?
  • Can emotional regulation be integrated (as in Show Me Your Colors) to connect cognitive and affective resilience?
  • What metrics best capture “orientation growth” over time?
